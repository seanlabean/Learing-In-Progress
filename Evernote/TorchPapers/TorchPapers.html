# Papers similar to my efforts (FAMILIARIZE WITH THESE)
  • Rahner, D.; Pellegrini, E.; Glover, S.; Klessen, R. (2017) Winds and radiation in unison: a new semi-analytic feedback model for cloud dissolution https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2017MNRAS.470.4453R/abstract
  • Rahner, D.; Pellegrini, E.; Glover, S.; Klessen, R. (2019) WARPFIELD 2.0: feedback-regulated minimum star formation efficiencies of giant molecular clouds https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2019MNRAS.483.2547R/abstract
  • Dale, J. E. et al. (2014) Before the first supernova: combined effects of H II regions and winds on molecular clouds https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2014MNRAS.442..694D/abstract
    • Info:
      SPH star formation simulation.  Self-gravitating clouds 1e4, 1e5, 1e6 solar mass; stellar physics - ionizing radiation, momentum driving winds
    • Question:
      How does the addition of stellar winds (accompanying photoionization) affect the morphology of GMCs and SFE/SFR
    • Method:
      See below
    • Conclusion:
      • Winds have low effect on cold gas/star formation progress except for when OB stars are initially deeply embedded
      • Winds have substantial effect on morphology of HII regions
        1. In early stages (while HII still actively breaking out of filamentary structure) winds help clear initial cavity to then be ionized by radiation. Results in spherical HII regions (seen in Wood & Churchwell 1989) rather than multilobed cavities.
        2. Later stages, winds enforce a ~10pc central hole within the HII region.
      • In low escape velocity clouds, winds & photoionization were able to unbind up to 50% of the natal gas.
      • SFE still order of magnitude too high
  • Dale, J. E. et al. (2012a) Ionizing Feedback from massive stars in massive clusters - II. Disruption of bound clusters by photoionization https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2012MNRAS.424..377D/abstract
    • Info:
      SPH star formation simulation. Self-gravitating clouds 1e4, 1e5, 1e6 solar mass; stellar physics - ionizing radiation
    • Question: 
      To what degree does photoionization of H2 clouds (due to massive star formation) disrupt the cloud or alter star formation process.
    • Method:
      SPH simulation 1e6 particles —> sink particles represent star clusters (except in 1e4 solar mass sims where sinks are individual stars)
      Thermodynamics handled by piecewise continuous multi gamma equation of state
    • Conclusion:
      • Photoionization has little effect (especially on massive/dense gas clouds) for two reasons
        1. High density prohibits deep entry of ionizing radiation into the cloud
        2. Ionized Hydrogen has a sound speed of 10 km/s, meaning the maximum rate of expansion for an HII region is the same. A high mass cloud will intrinsically have a higher escape velocity and thus is less likely to fall apart via this mechanism.
      • Little effect on star formation rate (not modeled?)

#Sink Particles
  • Federrath, C. et al. (2010): Modeling Collapse and Accretion in Turbulent Gas Clouds: Implementation and Comparison of Sink Particles in AMR and SPH
    http://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.1088/0004-637X/713/1/269/pdf
    • Info:
      Sinks originally created by Matthew Bate (Bate+1995) in SPH
    • Question:
      How to accurately implement sink particles in an AMR simulation? What conditions are needed?
    • Method:
      Sink particles are absolutely necessary to reduce computation strain and speed up simulations. A simple density threshold requirement is not sufficient; need gas “bound-ness”, G_pot min check, convergent flow.
    • Conclusion:
      Additional sink formation checks are vital for mirroring SPH behavior. Methods verified with checks of circular/elliptical orbits, Bonner-Ebert sphere collapse, rotating cloud core (Boss & Bodenheimer 1979; Bate & Burkert 1997)
  • Sormani M. C. et al. (2016): A simple method to convert sink particles into stars
    https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2017MNRAS.466..407S/abstract
    • Question:
      How to determine radiative, winds, other stellar properties of sink particles representing groups of stars.
    • Method:
      When sink forms, assign an appropriate IMF distribution for the given environment. Choose stars to constitute the mass of the sink while also abiding by the IMF. As sink accretes, assign more stars following IMF.
    • Conclusion:
      TORCH uses this method in a reverse way. Rather than assigning stellar properties to the sink, mass from the sink is removed as it accretes enough mass to form a star sampled from the IMF. Then the star is placed on the grid and in AMUSE.

#Initial Mass Function and how it can be used to create stars in simulations
  • Kroupa, P. (2002): Initial Mass Functions of Stars: Evidence for Uniformity in Variable Systems
    https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2002Sci...295...82K/abstract
  • Sormani, M. C. et al. (2017): A Simple Method to Convert Sink Particles into Stars
    https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2017MNRAS.466..407S/abstract
  • Krumholz et al. (2016): What Physics Determines the Peak of the IMF? Insights from the Structures of Cores in Radiation-Magnetohydrodynamics Simulations.
    https://arxiv.org/pdf/1603.04557.pdf
    • Simulation Analysis of Meyers+14 (1e3 solar mass clouds w/ sinks, radiative transfer, B-fields)
    • Question: What physics is the dominant limiting factor when it comes to star formation?
      • Accretion through brown-dwarf mass-range, why stop at 0.2 solar mass (peak of IMF)?
    • Method:
      • Analyzing data output from Meyers+14 simulations 
      • Physics included - gravity, magnetohydrodynamics, turbulence, radiative transfer (including from young stars), protostellar outflows.
    • Considerations:
      • How does gas density and turbulence affect the results: no simulations in the literature 
    • Conclusions:
      • Core densities around forming stars reach an equilibrium state and accretion appears to be stabilized by radiative output of the forming star.
      • In protostellar objects, the amount of gas around it that too warm to fragment is several times more massive than the object itself. Implying that accretion must eventually occur unless an outside disturbance is introduced.
      • As protostars climb into the stellar mass range (to 0.2 solar masses), the star’s ability to thermally surpress fragmentation of surrounding gas proportionally diminishes (only supporting a few % of star’s mass) thereby halting accretion and further stellar growth.

  • Fedderath, C. (2018) [Physics Today Issue June 2018, pp. 38 - 42): The turbulent formation of stars
    • Turbulence!
    • Modes of Turbulence:
      • Solenoidal (no divergence) - ex. Shear, magnetotoroidal instability.
      • Compressive (no curl) - ex. Accretion, SN shocks. (Much more efficient at seeding star formation; by an order of magnitude)
    • Turbulence sources are usually combinations of the two modes
      • Ex. Stellar jets, as they punch through the ISM, generate shear along their inner & outer edges of the jet: a solenoidal mode. Then, the front edge of the jet, the bow shock produced drives compressive modes.
    • Jets from single stars > jets from tight binaries > jets from wide binaries
